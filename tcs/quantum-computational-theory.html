Quantum Computational Theory

What is Quantum Computational Theory

Quantum Computational Theory combines the fields of physics, mathematics, and computer science. Borrowing applied and theortical concepts from each field, we can define what problems can be solved on a model of quantum computation using an algorithm and how efficiently they can be solved or to what degree. The reason this is important is because Quantum Computational Theory allows us to explore the theoretical possibilites of Quantum Computing and both formally and accuratley define what is and what is not possible in Quantum Computing.

We will first start by defining the theoretical models that are unique and important to the field of quantum computational theory. Then, we will learn about the field of quantum complexity theory. Let's begin.

Quantum Automata Theory and Formal Languages

Quantum Turing Machine

Formal Definition

QTM

\(\delta\) Function

A Quantum Turing Machine QTM can be expressed similarily to a traditional Turing Machine TM with all components reformulated canonically except for the transition function \(\delta\). Below, is the formal definition for a QTM.

Quantum Turing Machine is a 7-tuple:

\((Q, \Sigma, \Gamma, \delta, q_{0}, q_{accept}, q_{reject})\)

where \(Q, \Sigma, \Gamma\) are all finite sets and:

  • \(Q\) is a set of states
  • \(\Sigma\) is an alphabet not containing the blank symbol \(\sqcup\)
  • \(\Gamma\) is the tape alphabet, where \(\sqcup \in \Gamma\) and \(\Sigma \subseteq \Gamma\). The tape is assumed to be two-way infinite, with squares indexed by the set of integers \(\mathbb{Z}\)
  • \(\delta\) is a transition function described as \(\delta : Q \times \Gamma \rightarrow \mathbb{C}^{Q \times \Gamma \times \left\{-1, +1\right\}}\)
  • \(q_{0} \in Q\) is the initial state
  • \(q_{accept} \in Q\) is the accept state
  • \(q_{reject} \in Q\) is the reject state, where \(q_{reject} \neq q_{accept}\)

Deriving the Quantum Turing Machine

Superposition \(\phi\)

\(C\) numbers

First lets be take a look at each part of a Quantum Turing Machine QTM that can be expressed similarily to a traditional Turing Machine TM. The set of states \(Q\) in our QTM can be defined as \(Q = \left\{1,...,m\right\}\). Our tape alphabet, or the set containing the elements that the head of the QTM can read and write to, is defined as \(\Gamma = \left\{0,...,k-1\right\}\). Our tape, as stated, is assumed to be two-way infinite. Meaning the head can traverse the tape infinitley to the right and to the left as the tape goes on forever to the left and right. Our tape is indexed by the set of integers \(\mathbb{Z}\). Our head starts at index \(0\). Since, our set of indexes for the tape are the elemets of \(\mathbb{Z}\), which contains the infinite set of negative integers and positve integers, our head can traverse the tape infintley right and left. So, by defining the location of elements in our tape by the elements of the integers of the set \(\mathbb{Z}\), the tape can be two-way infinite.

As mentioned, the transition function \(\delta\) of the QTM cannot be reformulated canonically to the transition function of a TM. Thus, we will start with \(\delta\) of a TM and evolve the transition function for the QTM. The \(\delta\) of a traditional TM can be defined as:

\(\delta : Q \times \Gamma \rightarrow Q \times \Gamma \times \left\{L, R\right\}\)

The first evolution of \(\delta\) is changing \(\left\{L, R\right\}\) to \(\left\{-1, +1\right\}\):

\(\delta : Q \times \Gamma \rightarrow Q \times \Gamma \times \left\{-1, +1\right\}\)

Now that we have made the first evolution to \(\delta\) we should describe the assumptions for \(\delta\). The assumptions for \(\delta\) are the following.

The domain for \(\delta\) is in the state \(p \in Q\) and the tape head is reading a square from the tape that holds a symbol \(a \in \Gamma\). It it also the case that:

\(\delta(p,a)=(q, b, D)\)

where \(q \in Q\), \(b \in \Gamma\), and \(D \in \left\{-1, +1\right\}\).

Under these assumptions, our machine will do the following in one step.

In one step, the machine will change the current state from \(p\) to \(q\), overwrite the current scanned tape square from the symbol \(a\) to \(b\), and move the tape head in the direction \(D\). Again, \(D=\left\{-1,+1\right\}\), where \(-1\) means the tape head moves to the left and \(+1\) means the tape head moves to the right.

In our formal definition, the codomain for the transition funciton \(\delta\) of a QTM is:

\(\delta : Q \times \Gamma \rightarrow \mathbb{C}^{Q \times \Gamma \times \left\{-1, +1\right\}}\)

Lets \(\mathbb{C}\) be the set of complex numbers. \(\forall \alpha \in \mathbb{C}\) there exists some deterministic algorithm that computes the real and imaginary parts of \(\alpha \leq 2^{-n}\) in polynomial time in \(n\).

Inner-product Space

Similiarily to our traditional TM, our QTM has configurations as well. The configurations for our QTM is comprised of the initial configuration and final configurations.

Let \(S\) be the inner-product space of finite complex linear combinations of configurations for our QTM \(M\) with Eucledian normal form. An inner-product space is a real vector space or a complex vector space. An example of a vector space is \(V\) over the field \(F\) together with an inner-product, that is a map:

\(\left\langle\cdot,\cdot\right\rangle\ : V \times V \rightarrow F\)

A QTM's vector space for S is a finite space of configurations that are produced from the combination of complex liner combinations where each combination is defined by the innner-product \(\delta : Q \times \Gamma \rightarrow \mathbb{C}^{Q \times \Gamma \times \left\{-1, +1\right\}}\).

We call each element \(\phi \in S\) a superposition. Where each superposition is the codmain of linear combination of configurations for some initial configuration.

Time Evolution Operator

Our QTM \(M\) defines a linear operator \(U_{M} : S \rightarrow S\), called the time eveloution operator of \(M\). The time evolution operator of \(M\) is:

If \(M\) starts in configruation \(c\) with current state \(p\) and scanned symbol \(\delta\), then after one step \(M\) will be in superposition of configurations \(\psi = \sum_{i} \alpha_{i} c_{i}\). Each non-zero \(\alpha_{i} \) corresponds to a transition \(\delta(p, \sigma, \tau, q, d)\), and \(c_{i}\) is the new configuration that results in applying this transition to \(c\). By extending this map to all elements of space \(S\) through linearity gives the linear time evolution operator \(U_{M}\). This defenition comes from "Quantum complexity theory", Ethan Bernstein and Umesh Vazirani
