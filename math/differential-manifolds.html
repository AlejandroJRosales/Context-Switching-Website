What is a manifold?

One definition of a manifold with a topology:

\((M, \tau_{M}, A)\)
\(A: Atlas (U_{\alpha}, P_{\alpha})\)
\(p \in M : U_{p} \in \tau_{M} : \exists P_{p} : U \rightarrow P_{p}(U) \subset \mathbb{R}^{d}\)

This says is that we have a point \(p\) that is an element of our topological space \(M\). Such that, our open subset \(U_{p}\) is an element of our topology in our topological space \(\tau_{M}\) that has our point \(p\) in \(U\). i.e. from the set of neighboors or set of elements that are apart of our topology \(\tau_{M}\), the open subset \(U\) we choose needs the point \(p\) as an element of that set. Such that, there exists some \(\exists\) homeomorphic function \(P\) for our point \(p\) that when we pass our open subset \(U\) through the function, the range is a set that is apart or a proper subset of Eucledian space \(\mathbb{R}^{d}\). Our atlas \(A\) is defined as an ordered pair with a set of open subsets \(U_{\alpha}\) a set of their respective homeomorphisms \(P_{\alpha}\).

Another definition of a manifold without a topology:

Let \(M\) be a topological space. A chart in \(M\) consits of an open subset \(U \subset M\) and a homoemorphism \(h\) of \(U\) onto an open subset of \(R^{m}\). A \(C^{r}\) atlas on \(M\) is a collection \(\left\{U_{\alpha}, h_{\alpha}\right\}\) of charts such that the \(U_{\alpha}\) cover \(M\) and \(h_{\beta}h_{\alpha}^{-1}\), the transition maps, are \(C^{r}\) maps on \(h_{\alpha}(U_{\alpha}\cap U_{\beta})\).

Similar to what we have above, except here our open subset \(U\) is a proper subset of our topological space \(M\) instead of a topology that is an additional structure in the topological space.

Manifolds: Definition Analysis

First: Lets look at elements in our ordered-pair that define our manifold:

\((M, \tau_{M}, A)\)

\(M\) Topological Space

Topologoical vs Eucledian Space

Lets break this down. First we will start with getting an idea of what a topological space is, since this is one of the first parts of our defenition of a manifold. A topological space is roughly speaking where the closness of the points in the space cannot be numerically defined, unlike in Eucledian space. What is Eucledian space? If you recall, in school we would plot a point in 2-d space using (x, y) and an x and y axis with dashes along the side to help define the space between values. This is 2-dimensional Eucledian space. Or written as \(\mathbb{R}^{2}\). Of course, our Eucledian space is not limited to 2 dimensions. We can use similar descriptors for 3-d space, 11-space, and any number of dimensions, or \(n\)-d Eucledian space \(\mathbb{R}^{n}\). However, continuing with 2-d space, using our numbered x and y axis and our points with coordinates, we can figure out the numerical value that represents how close one point is to other. However, a topological space, unlike our familiar Eucledian space, is more generalized than that. Rather, our space does not have "numbered axis" to define one points relation to another. Rather our space is represented as a set of points whose elements are called points. And in this set that defines our topological space, we have a structure called a topolgy. This topology is defined as a set of neighbourhoods for every point that, by examining the other points or elements that are in this neighborhood set, it gives an idea of closeness or relation of one point to another. So, we have a set of points that defines what makes up our topological space, and a structure called a topology that is defined as set of neighboords (neighboorhoods are represents as sets too). So now we have a rough idea of the first part of our short version: "...is a topological space...". Mathematically, the part we examined was: Let \(M\) be a topological space.

\(\tau_{M}\) Topology

Open Subsets

Neighboorhoods

A topology, roughly speaking, is an additional structure to a topolgical space that can be defined as a set of neighboorhoods. A neighboorhood is closely related to an open subset. This is why in out definition when we take an element from our toplogy we get an open subset. i.e. \(U_{p} \in \tau_{M}\). Basically, a neighboorhood of a point from a topological space is a set of points that contains that point and the points for which we can move around in any direction and still be in our set or neighboorhood. This is why our point \(p\) that we have is an element of \(U\). Since when we take a point we want to examine a slice of our topology that contains the point we are examining and not some random point. We do this so we can keep properly traverse through our topology and keep a list of all of the chart and their respective homeomoprhisms. This allows us to determine if all elements or open subsets in our topology, when sent through their respective homeomorphisms, are apart of eucledian space \(R^{d}\). i.e. if parts of our topological space \(M\) examined locally are apart of eucledian space \(R^{d}\).

Final Piece

Finally: Lets talk about the way all of these parts fit in respect to eachother:

\(p \in M : U_{p} \in \tau_{M} : \exists P_{p} : U \rightarrow P_{p}(U) \subset \mathbb{R}^{d}\)

This says is that we have a point \(p\) that is an element of our topological space \(M\). Such that, our open subset \(U_{p}\) is an element of our topology in our topological space \(\tau_{M}\) that has our point \(p\) in \(U\). i.e. from the set of neighboors or set of elements that are apart of our topology \(\tau_{M}\), the open subset \(U\) we choose needs the point \(p\) as an element of that set. Such that, there exists some \(\exists\) homeomorphic function \(P\) for our point \(p\) that when we pass our open subset \(U\) through the function, the range is a set that is apart or a proper subset of Eucledian space \(\mathbb{R}^{d}\).

i.e. there is a function that when we send through apart of our topological space, we can get something that is apart of Eucledian space. And, there is an atlas that is a collection of all of the charts and respective transition functions to translate them to Eucledian space, for the topological space. That, when we put all of our chart together in the atlas, we recieve a correct version of our topological space.
