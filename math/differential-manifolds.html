Definitions

Formal Definition

What is a manifold? One definition:

\((M, \tau_{M}, A)\)
\(A: Atlas (U_{\alpha}, P_{\alpha})\)
\(p \in M : U_{p} \in \tau_{M} : \exists P_{p} : U \rightarrow P_{p}(U) \subset \mathbb{R}^{d}\)

Another definition:

Let \(M\) be a topological space. A chart in \(M\) consits of an open subset \(U \subset M\) and a homoemorphism \(h\) of \(U\) onto an open subset of \(R^{m}\). A \(C^{r}\) atlas on \(M\) is a collection \(\left\{U_{\alpha}, h_{\alpha}\right\}\) of charts such that the \(U_{\alpha}\) cover \(M\) and \(h_{\beta}h_{\alpha}^{-1}\), the transition maps, are \(C^{r}\) maps on \(h_{\alpha}(U_{\alpha}\cap U_{\beta})\).

Informal Definition

Ok, but I dont speak squiggles. Using no math notation, what is a manifold? Translate what the math notation above is saying.

The short version: a manifold is a topological space that locally resembles Eucliden space.

Medium version: an \(n\)-dimensional manifold, or \(n\)-manifold, is a topological space where each point has a neighboorhood that is homeomorphic to an open subset on \(n\)-dimensional Euclidean space.

Long version: Lets break this down. First we will start with getting an idea of what a topological space is, since this is one of the first parts of our defenition of a manifold. A topological space is roughly speaking where the closness of the points in the space cannot be numerically defined, unlike in Eucledian space. What is Eucledian space? If you recall, in school we would plot a point in 2-d space using (x, y) and an x and y axis with dashes along the side to help define the space between values. This is 2-dimensional Eucledian space. Or written as \(\mathbb{R}^{2}\). Of course, our Eucledian space is not limited to 2 dimensions. We can use similar descriptors for 3-d space, 11-space, and any number of dimensions, or \(n\)-d Eucledian space \(\mathbb{R}^{n}\). However, continuing with 2-d space, using our numbered x and y axis and our points with coordinates, we can figure out the numerical value that represents how close one point is to other. However, a topological space, unlike our familiar Eucledian space, is more generalized than that. Rather, our space does not have "numbered axis" to define one points relation to another. Rather our space is represented as a set of points whose elements are called points. And in this set that defines our topological space, we have a structure called a topolgy. This topology is defined as a set of neighbourhoods for every point that, by examining the other points or elements that are in this neighborhood set, it gives an idea of closeness or relation of one point to another. So, we have a set of points that defines what makes up our topological space, and a structure called a topology that is defined as set of neighboords (neighboorhoods are represents as sets too). So now we have a rough idea of the first part of our short version: "...is a topological space...". Mathematically, the part we examined was: Let \(M\) be a topological space.
