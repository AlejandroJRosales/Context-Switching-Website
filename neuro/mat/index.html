Modular-Network of Adaptive Topologies

Website for Modular-network of Adapting Topologies simulation: MAT

Modular-Network of Adaptive Topologies MAT is a biologically grounded spiking neural network simulator that simulates the brain as seven anatomically and molecularly motivated regions. Each modulated network is populated by neurons determined by their neurotransmitter identity. Six neurotransmitters are represented: glutamate, GABA, acetylcholine, dopamine, serotonin, and norepinephrine.

Introduction

The brain is a collection of functionally specialized regions of dense, chemically heterogeneous connectivity, where different regions use different neurotransmitters, fire at different rates, and maintain excitatory-to-inhibitory balances tuned for their computational roles. Simulating these micro/macro architectures and dynamics, that are qualitatively recognizable to neuroscientists, is the motivation for the MAT neural simulation.

Many large-scale neural network simulators either abstract away chemical identity entirely, treating all synapses as generic signed weights or model individual ion channels with an implementation that makes scaled simulations computationally prohibitive. This causes a gap between biologically tractable between capturing kinetics, molecular and chemical biology and neuroanatomy, histology, and abstract topology of the brain.

MAT is designed to occupy the middle ground between these extremes. It models the brain as seven anatomically motivated regions. These regions are the prefrontal cortex, motor cortex, occipital cortex, temporal/parietal cortex, parietal association cortex, thalamus, and brainstem. Here, each region is populated by neurons or nodes whose intrinsic properties and spontaneous drive are determined by their neurotransmitter identity. The excitatory-to-inhibitory portion follows widely cited estimates that approximately 20% of cortical neurons are GABAergic interneurons, with a reduced proportion in subcortical structures. Stochastic processes, such as membrane voltage fluctuations, probabilistic synaptic vesicle release failure, and neurotransmitter-specific spontaneous firing, are incorporated at the level of individual neurons, reproducing the fundamental unreliability that characterizes real neural transmission.

Beyond its role as a network dynamics simulation, MAT is the first component of a longer-term effort to construct embodied, goal-driven artificial intelligence in which adaptive behavior arises from pressure on a biologically organized substrate rather explicit reward engineering. This requires MAT's neurotransmitter model, i.e. the tonic drives of dopaminergic, noradrenergic, and serotonergic systems, to later serve as the mico-macro structure of neuroevolutionary iterations of the architecture.

Methodology

Anatomical Organization

MAT partitions its neural population into seven modules intended to one day represent broad functional anatomy similar to that of the mammalian brain. Where the occipital cortex receives sensory input, the temporal/parietal and parietal association areas integrate and route information, the prefrontal cortex imposes top-down regulatory control and governs planning, the motor cortex drives output, the thalamus relays signals between cortical regions, and the brainstem provides the tonic neuromodulatory drive.

Within each region, neurons are topologically distributed and form local synapses or edges at a region-specific connection probability. The architecture reflects that connectivity is denser within cortical regions than between them. Cross-regional projections are formed at lower probabilities. Note, currently, every directed pair of regions is guaranteed a minimum number of bidirectional connections, consistent with the observation that virtually all cortical regions maintain at least weak reciprocal connectivity

The thalamus is assigned both a high local connection probability and the broadest cross-regional projection profile of any module, thus reflecting its role as the primary relay hub of the brain. This causes nearly all ascending sensory signals and a large fraction of corticocortical communication pass through or are modulated by thalamic circuits. The brainstem projects broadly across all regions, consistent with the diffuse, volumetric character of monoaminergic neuromodulation from the locus coeruleus, raphe nuclei, and ventral tegmental area.

Excitatory and Inhibitory Balance

Consistent with established cortical neuroanatomy, approximately 20% of neurons in each cortical region are modeled as inhibitory interneurons, while 80% are excitatory projection neurons. This ratio is reduced to 10% in the thalamus and brainstem, reflecting the comparatively smaller GABAergic interneuron populations in these subcortical structures.

Meanwhile, all inhibitory neurons release GABA exclusively, with synaptic projections that hyperpolarize their targets. This provides the feedforward and feedback inhibition essential for preventing runaway excitation and shaping temporal dynamics such as oscillatory rhythms and winner-take-all competition between neural populations.

Neuron Model

Individual neurons are modeled as leaky integrate-and-fire (LIF) units to mimic integrate-to-threshold behavior of cortical neurons without the computational overhead of full compartmental models. At each simulation step, a neuron accumulates weighted synaptic input from all incoming neurons that fired in the preceding step. Note, each neuron accumlation is subject to stochastic release failure at each synapse. When accumulated input some threshold of the neuron, it causes an action potential, followed by an absolute refractory period of some time step. Such that, fast glutamatergic neurons recover quickly, whereas monoaminergic neurons have prolonged refractory periods.

Stochastic Noise

Three independent stochastic mechanisms introduce biological variability into the network.

Membrane Voltage Fluctuations

Studies have shown that cortical neurons operate in a high-conductance state where synaptic input from thousands of background inputs causes continuous, approximately Gaussian fluctuations in membrane potential. MAT approximates this regime by adding independent Gaussian noise to each neuron's input at every simulation step. At biologically plausible amplitudes, this allows neurons near threshold to fire stochastically, contributing to the trial-to-trial variability of cortical spiking statistics.

Synaptic Vesicle Release Failure

A single presynaptic action potential does not guarantee neurotransmitter release. At many central synapses, vesicle fusion probability is well below unity, and release failure rates of 50-90% have been reported at individual cortical boutons. In MAT, each synaptic transmission event fails independently with a default probability of 7%, introducing signal degradation that scales with network sparsity.

Neurotransmitter-Specific Spontaneous Activity

Many neuron types exhibit spontaneous subthreshold depolarization where locus coeruleus noradrenergic neurons discharge tonically at 1-5 Hz, raphe serotonergic neurons maintain slow rhythmic baseline activity, and VTA dopaminergic neurons display both tonic and phasic firing modes. MAT mimics this behaviour by assigning each neuron a spontaneous firing probability composed of a global background term and a neurotransmitter-specific additive component. The latter is largest for noradrenergic neurons and negligible for glutamatergic and GABAergic neurons, ensuring that the brainstem module has persistent background activity.

Neurotransmitter Model

A central feature of MAT is its per-neuron neurotransmitter identity, which jointly determines three aspects of a neuron's physiological behavior. 1. the gain with which its output is weighted at postsynaptic targets, reflecting differences in receptor conductance and coupling efficiency. 2. the duration of its post-spike refractory period, reflecting transmitter-specific kinetics. 3. its intrinsic spontaneous firing drive. These parameters are summarized in the table below.

Neurotransmitter Gain Refractory Offset Spontaneous Boost Inhibitory
Glutamate 1.00 +0 0.000 No

Synaptic Architecture

Synaptic weights are initialized randomly, with the sign determined by the neurotransmitter identity of the presynaptic neuron. No plasticity rule is applied in the current version. Meaning, currently, the network operates in a fixed-weight regime to allow characterization of spontaneous and evoked dynamics before learning mechanisms are introduced.

Sensory Input: Visual Pathway Coupling

For now, the model is instantiated as a closed loop. However, upon access, MAT couples its occipital module to a live camera feed. This is motivated by the anatomy of the primary visual pathway, in which retinal output is relayed through the lateral geniculate nucleus of the thalamus to primary visual cortex.

For each frame, the camera image is converted to a grayscale, luminance map normalized to \([0, 1]\). This luminance signal is delivered to occipital neurons as a sustained excitatory current, with each neuron receiving input scaled by an individually assigned sensitivity coefficient. When no camera input is present, the occipital module operates on internally generated spontaneous and synaptically driven activity alone, mimicking spontaneous cortical dynamics during visual deprivation or resting state.
