Hippocampus: An Overview

Anatomy and Histolog

Section Reference: Cliques and cavities in the human connectome by Ann E. Sizemore, Chad Giusti, Ari Kahn, Jean M. Vettel, Richard F. Betzel, and Danielle S. Bassett \(^{[1]}\)

The hippocampus contains two parts, the Cornu ammonis and the dentate gyrus, where the hippocampal sulcus separates both parts. Both parts curve into each other and below the sulcus lies the subiculum. Since the hippocampus is a part of the allocortex or archicortex, there exists a zone that separates the hippocampus from the neocortex. The hippocampal set is the entire set of hippocampal formation. This formation is comprised by some anatomists into four main sections: Hippocampus proper, dentate gyrus DG, subiculum, and the entorhinal area EC. This entire set of hippocampus zones is called the hippocamapal formation. The hippocampus is supplied blood by the posterior Cerebral artery and is covered by choroid plexus.

Histology tells us that the hippocampus proper or cornus ammonis is divided into four histological domains: CA1, CA2, CA3, and CA4. Across from the CA1 is the subiculum, which is a formation connecting the hippocampus to the enthornial cortex in the ventricle. Where the enthornial cortex inputs to the dentata gryus and plays an important role in pattern recognition and encoding of memories.
